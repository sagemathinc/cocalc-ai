# CoCalc.ai Landing Page — Copy Playbook

> How we write public-site copy so it reads natural and credible to a Western professional
> audience and lands the value for BOTH the technical user and the economic buyer. Distilled
> from an adversarially-verified research pass (Dunford / Copyhackers / Evil Martians' study
> of 100+ devtool pages / OSP), 2026-06-17. Use alongside `docs/landing-page-brief.md`.

## Principles (verified)

1. **Copy flows from positioning, not from a blank page.** Write from the Brief's
   differentiated value and audience — not a generic category claim. (Dunford: positioning is
   the precursor to messaging; basing copy on differentiated value prevents drift.)
2. **Feature → "so what," but keep the feature.** Engineers want the concrete capability;
   *everyone* needs the outcome it enables. So state the feature **and** add the outcome —
   don't replace one with the other. (Dunford: "customers care what features can do for them";
   but for a technical reader, dropping the feature loses trust.)
3. **A benefit-led hero is legitimate — don't force concreteness or a problem-lead.** The
   "say it in 3-4 words," "problem-led heroes win," and "concrete always beats abstract" rules
   were **refuted** in verification. A clear benefit + a grounding subhead works. Lead with the
   value; let the subhead say who it's for and prove it.
4. **Be specific where you can; never summarize when you can be concrete.** Real numbers,
   real workflow examples, voice-of-customer phrasing read as credible; vague summaries read as
   filler. (Copyhackers.) Use proven headline structures instead of inventing each from scratch.
5. **Strip salesy language and unverified superlatives — they ERODE credibility with
   engineers.** Banned: *revolutionary, best-in-class, game-changing, world-class, AI-powered,
   cutting-edge, seamless, enterprise-ready,* and the generic *scalable, powerful, easy.* Two
   universal devtool-page rules: **"no salesy BS"** and **"clever and simple wins."** Earn any
   strong claim with a number, a demo, or a concrete example.

## The dual-audience technique: LAYER, don't average

Serve the practitioner and the budget-holder on the **same** page by stacking, not blending:
- **One line of context per feature/section:** *what problem it solves · who it's for · why it
  matters.* The technical detail stays (for the user); the context line lets the buyer in.
- **Match proof to audience:** practitioners trust usage/adoption signals and concrete
  workflow examples; decision-makers trust logos, sector references, and the operating-model
  story. Put both on the page, aimed at their respective readers.
- **Two-CTA hero:** one **primary** conversion CTA (*Start on CoCalc.ai*) + a clearly
  **subordinate** low-commitment CTA (docs / compare / explore). Co-equal CTAs hurt conversion.

## The reusable unit: the Value Case (OSP)

For a feature or product section: **Benefit + Challenge + Solution** — "the technical answer to
a business question." Map it both directions from one idea:
- **Up (why):** feature → outcome → business value (for the buyer).
- **Down (how):** the claim → the concrete technical truth that backs it (for the engineer).
This keeps the two audiences seeing *consistent, mutually-derived* messaging, not two pitches.

## Section checklist (run on any page before shipping)

- [ ] Does the hero state a clear **benefit**, with a subhead that says who it's for + grounds it?
- [ ] Does each feature pair the **concrete capability** with its **outcome** (the "so what")?
- [ ] Is there **one line of context** (problem / who / why) so a non-specialist can enter?
- [ ] Are there **zero** banned superlatives, and is every strong claim **earned** with proof?
- [ ] Is the copy **specific** (real examples/numbers) rather than summarized?
- [ ] **One** primary CTA, with any secondary clearly subordinate?
- [ ] Does it sound like it was **written by someone who does the work** — would a skeptical
      researcher/engineer nod, not wince?

## What we deliberately do NOT assume (refuted in verification)

- Problem-led heroes do **not** reliably outperform benefit/action heroes.
- There is **no** "first 3-4 words" rule, and concrete copy does **not** always beat abstract
  benefit copy. Pick per case; don't dogmatize.
- Voice-of-customer research is valuable but **not** an absolute prerequisite to writing.
